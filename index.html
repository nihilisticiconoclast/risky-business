Portfolio Summary

Heads up — this is a built-in sample portfolio, not yours. So that the dashboard has something to show out of the box, it ships with a demo basket called the “Sample Tech Portfolio.” You haven’t added anything — there isn’t an “add my portfolio” button yet. The holdings live in the data pipeline (the SAMPLE_HOLDINGS list in python/fetch_real_data.py) and currently hold 100 AAPL, 100 MSFT, 50 GOOGL and 20 AMZN, weighted by market value. Edit those rows and re-run the fetcher to model your own holdings.

The four numbers below treat that whole basket as a single investment and summarise how it behaved over the past ~10 years: how much it grew, how bumpy the ride was, whether the reward justified the risk, and the worst drop it suffered along the way. Use the Portfolio tab to compare individual stocks, or Stock Analysis to drill into one.

What these numbers mean

Annualized Return
The portfolio’s average growth rate expressed as a yearly figure (daily returns scaled up by 252 trading days). It tells you about reward only — not how risky earning it was.
Annualized Volatility
How much the returns swing around that average, stated per year. Higher means a bumpier, less predictable ride. Roughly 15–25% is typical for a single large-cap stock; a diversified basket is usually lower.
Sharpe Ratio
Return earned per unit of risk — the single best “bang for your buck” number. Above 1 is good, above 2 is excellent. It lets you compare investments fairly even when their volatilities differ.
Max Drawdown
The largest peak-to-trough fall over the period — i.e. the worst loss you’d have lived through if you’d bought at the high and held to the low. Shown as a negative percentage; closer to zero is safer.

Individual Stock Risk Metrics

Pick any of the tracked stocks to see its personal risk-and-reward profile, followed by its full price history and its day-to-day returns. This is the place to ask “how has this one name actually behaved?” — the cards below quantify it, and the charts let you eyeball the trend and the turbulence.

Reading the cards

Annualized Return & Volatility
Same idea as on the summary, but for this single stock: its average yearly growth, and how much that growth bounces around.
Sharpe Ratio
This stock’s reward-per-unit-of-risk. Handy for asking whether a racier name actually pays you for the extra white-knuckle moments.
Max Drawdown
Its worst peak-to-trough decline over the period — the deepest hole it dug before recovering.
VaR (95%)
Value at Risk. On a typical day, 19 days out of 20 the loss stays smaller than this figure — it’s a quick read on the “bad but not catastrophic” daily move you should expect.
Beta
How tightly the stock moves with the overall market (here, the equal-weighted average of every tracked stock). 1.0 means it moves in step; above 1 amplifies the market’s swings, below 1 cushions them.
Observations
How many trading days of data went into these numbers — more days means a more reliable estimate.

Portfolio Analysis

These charts line up every tracked stock side by side so you can compare them at a glance across the four risk dimensions. Everything is grouped and coloured by sector, so patterns jump out that single numbers hide — which sectors are calm versus jumpy, which pay best for their risk, and which have historically fallen hardest. All charts are interactive: hover for exact values, click a sector in the legend to show/hide it, drag to zoom, double-click to reset.

Risk-Return Profile

Each dot is a stock, coloured by sector: horizontal = annualized return, vertical = volatility (risk). The sweet spot is the bottom-right — strong return for low risk. Dots up top took big swings to get there.

Volatility Comparison

Taller bars are bumpier, less predictable stocks. This is pure risk, ignoring whether the stock actually went up.

Sharpe Ratio Comparison

Taller bars delivered more return per unit of risk — the closest thing to an all-round “quality” score. Bars below zero lost money over the period.

Max Drawdown Comparison

Bars point down because drawdowns are losses. The longer the bar, the deeper the worst-case fall that stock has put investors through.

Build Your Own Portfolio

This is the real thing — your portfolio, not the sample. Add any of the 47 stocks, set how much weight each one carries, and the risk metrics and charts are recomputed live in your browser from ~10 years of daily prices. Nothing is saved or sent anywhere; weights are automatically normalised to 100%, so you can enter them as percentages, shares, or any relative numbers you like.

Growth of $10,000

How $10,000 invested at the start would have tracked, versus an equal-weight basket of all 47 stocks as a benchmark.

Sector Allocation

Your normalised weights grouped by sector — a quick read on how diversified (or concentrated) the mix is.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Project the portfolio forward by simulating thousands of possible futures and looking at the range of where it could land. Historical bootstrap resamples the portfolio's own past daily returns (keeping its real fat tails and skew); GBM assumes normally-distributed daily moves calibrated to its history. This is a distribution of possibilities, not a forecast — past behaviour doesn't guarantee the future.

Projected Value — percentile fan

The median outcome over time, with the 25th–75th and 5th–95th percentile bands. Wider bands = more uncertainty.

Distribution of Final Values

Where the simulated end values landed. The left tail is your downside risk.

Stock Compass

Point the compass and it ranks all tracked stocks by their current risk-and-reward, then suggests where the needle is pointing right now. It blends four signals from the live price history — risk-adjusted quality, recent momentum, longer-term trend, and stability — and reweights them to match your risk appetite.

Educational screen, not financial advice. This is a transparent, rules-based ranking of historical price data — it has no knowledge of company fundamentals, news, or valuations, and past behaviour doesn't predict the future. Always do your own research.
